1. Executive Summary

BioForge is a computational-experimental framework being developed by Ogaga Maxwell Okedi to address a persistent engineering bottleneck in industrial biotechnology: the failure of laboratory-optimized bioprocesses to translate reliably to pilot or production scale. By integrating Design of Experiments (DOE) methodology with Machine Learning (ML) predictive modeling, BioForge provides a structured, data-driven pipeline enabling researchers and process engineers to identify optimal fermentation conditions, predict biochemical yields with high fidelity, and generate actionable scale-up protocols.

The framework is substrate-agnostic by design — applicable to any fermentation system regardless of feedstock type. Initial validation was performed using data from Mr. Okedi's peer-reviewed flagship publication on citric acid production from yam peel waste, achieving a documented 49.1% yield improvement over the unoptimized baseline (28.90 g/l → 43.08 g/l). BioForge is intended to function as both a research tool and an open-source public resource for U.S. academic institutions, biorefineries, and contract manufacturing organizations seeking to reduce biochemical import dependence.

2. Problem Statement

2.1 The Lab-to-Scale Engineering Bottleneck

Biochemical production via microbial fermentation depends on a complex, multivariate set of process conditions — pH, temperature, dissolved oxygen, substrate concentration, nitrogen-to-carbon ratio, inoculum density, and fermentation duration. Standard laboratory practice optimizes these variables using one-factor-at-a-time (OFAT) experimentation: each parameter is varied independently while others are held constant. This approach cannot capture interaction effects between process variables — interactions that are often the dominant determinants of yield at scale.

The consequence is well-documented in the bioprocess engineering literature: conditions that maximize yield at 250 mL flask scale frequently fail to replicate performance in 10 L bioreactors, let alone at industrial scale. Each transition requires a new round of costly, time-consuming empirical optimization — a prohibitive barrier to commercialization for research groups and emerging biorefinery startups alike.

2.2 U.S. Import Dependence on Biochemicals

The failure to efficiently translate laboratory findings into scalable bioprocesses has real economic consequences for the United States. Despite possessing abundant agricultural feedstocks suitable for fermentation — including corn stover, sugarcane bagasse, soybean hulls, and food processing waste — the U.S. currently imports large volumes of biochemicals that could be domestically produced:

  • Citric acid: The U.S. imports approximately 170,000 metric tons annually, primarily from China, representing a significant supply chain vulnerability for the food, pharmaceutical, and industrial cleaning sectors.
  • Succinic acid: Approximately 59% of U.S. supply is sourced from China and South Korea, despite succinic acid being a platform chemical with broad polymer and specialty chemical applications.
  • Lactic acid: The U.S. market is dominated by imports from Thailand and China, even as domestic demand for biodegradable polylactic acid (PLA) bioplastics grows rapidly. North America accounts for 45% of global demand yet relies on foreign supply to meet it.

2.3 The Root Cause: Optimization Methodology

The primary technical barrier is not feedstock availability — it is the inadequacy of conventional optimization methods. Statistical approaches such as Response Surface Methodology exist but are rarely paired with predictive ML models that can generalize beyond the experimental design space. BioForge is designed to close this gap permanently, for any waste stream and any target product.

3. BioForge Solution Overview

3.1 Core Concept

BioForge integrates two complementary methodological traditions into a single accessible software framework:

  • Design of Experiments (DOE): Structured experimental design — Central Composite Design, Box-Behnken, Plackett-Burman, Full/Fractional Factorial — that captures variable interactions with a minimum number of experiments, replacing inefficient OFAT workflows.
  • Machine Learning Predictive Modeling: Supervised learning algorithms — Artificial Neural Networks (ANN), Adaptive Neuro-Fuzzy Inference Systems (ANFIS), Random Forest (RF), and Support Vector Machines (SVM) — trained on DOE-generated data to build high-fidelity yield prediction models that can be queried for optimization anywhere in the factor space.

3.2 Substrate Agnosticism

A deliberate design principle of BioForge is that it makes no assumptions about the specific fermentation substrate, microorganism, or target product. The framework operates on experimental data structured according to DOE principles and applies ML modeling to whatever input-output relationships are present. This means BioForge can be applied — without modification to core code — to citric acid from corn waste, succinic acid from soybean hulls, lactic acid from dairy whey, biodiesel from waste oils, or any other fermentation-based or catalytic bioprocess system.

Key Design Principle
BioForge is permanently available as an open-source tool to any U.S. research institution, national laboratory, or manufacturer — without requiring access to the developer or his laboratory. Once deployed, any institution can run its own waste stream through the platform and receive actionable optimization results independently.

4. Current Development Status

Development Status Statement
BioForge is currently in active development. The core optimization engine has been prototyped and validated using experimental data from the petitioner's flagship published study — "Biotechnological Conversion of Yam Peels for Enhanced Citric Acid Production" (Industrial Crops and Products, Impact Factor 6.2). This validation confirmed that the integrated DOE + ML pipeline can reproduce and extend experimental findings, generating optimization protocols consistent with the published 49.1% yield improvement result (28.90 g/l → 43.08 g/l citric acid). The repository is publicly accessible at https://github.com/ogaga-ai/BioForge.

4.1 Implemented and Validated Components

  • DOE Matrix Generation Engine: Automated generation of Central Composite Design (CCD), Box-Behnken Design (BBD), Plackett-Burman Design (PBD), and full/fractional factorial matrices for user-specified process variables, ranges, and center-point replicates.
  • ML Model Fitting Pipeline: End-to-end supervised learning workflow including data preprocessing, feature normalization (StandardScaler), Leave-One-Out cross-validation, and evaluation using R², RMSE, MAE, and SEP metrics — across ANN (tanh activation, MFFF architecture), Random Forest (200 estimators), Gradient Boosting, and SVR (RBF kernel).
  • Yield Prediction Interface: Programmatic query interface allowing input of arbitrary process condition vectors to retrieve predicted yield values with model confidence bounds.
  • Sensitivity Analysis Module: Permutation-based feature importance ranking that identifies which process variables most strongly influence yield — directly reproducing the Sobol sensitivity indices from the published study (Sodium Fluoride: 67.5%, Coconut Oil: 31.4%, EDTA: 9.3%).
  • Optimization Protocol Card Generator: Automated production of structured protocol reports summarizing optimal process conditions, predicted yield improvement over baseline, factor sensitivity ranking, and reproducibility information for any U.S. lab to implement independently.
  • Jupyter Notebook Interface: Fully documented interactive notebooks enabling step-by-step execution of the complete pipeline with integrated visualization outputs (EDA scatter plots, predicted vs. actual plots, response surface contour maps, sensitivity bar charts).

4.2 Components Currently In Progress

  • Hollow-Fiber Membrane Separation Module: Applies the Yang-Cussler mass transfer model (redesigned by Mr. Okedi as part of his graduate work at Florida A&M University) to model downstream product recovery at scale.
  • ANFIS Full Integration: Complete Adaptive Neuro-Fuzzy Inference System implementation matching the architecture from the published study (Sugeno-type, 27 rules, hybrid training, R² = 0.99880).
  • Web-Based User Interface: Browser-deployable interface (FastAPI/Streamlit) enabling non-programming users to configure DOE parameters, upload experimental data, and retrieve ML-optimized recommendations without writing code.
  • Substrate Library: Curated database of common U.S. agricultural waste streams with pre-parameterized DOE templates to accelerate adoption by U.S. labs and manufacturers.

7. Sample Workflow Demonstration — Citric Acid Validation Case

The following traces the end-to-end BioForge pipeline as validated against the published citric acid study data. This demonstration uses all 17 experimental runs from the published Box-Behnken Design matrix and reproduces the 49.1% yield improvement result.

INPUT: Yam peel agricultural waste (Dioscorea spp.)
    Variables: EDTA [0–0.30 g/l], Coconut Oil [0–5.0 %w/w], Sodium Fluoride [0–0.10 g/l]
    Fixed: Temp 30°C | Duration 6 days | Moisture 85% | Inoculum 10⁸ spores/ml

▶ DOE MATRIX: Box-Behnken Design — 17 runs + 5 center-point replicates
    Generated by BioForge DOEEngine.box_behnken(center_points=5)

▶ ML FITTING: 4 models trained, evaluated by Leave-One-Out cross-validation
    ANN (tanh, MFFF): R² = 0.99883 RMSE = 0.27072 g/l
    Random Forest: R² = 0.99+ LOO-RMSE evaluated
    Gradient Boosting: R² = 0.99+ LOO-RMSE evaluated
    SVR (RBF): R² = 0.99+ LOO-RMSE evaluated

▶ SENSITIVITY: Permutation importance (100 repeats) — matches published Sobol indices
    Rank 1: Sodium Fluoride 67.5% of yield variance
    Rank 2: Coconut Oil 31.4% of yield variance
    Rank 3: EDTA 9.3% of yield variance

▶ OPTIMUM: Grid search (50 points/factor) identifies global maximum
    EDTA = 0.30 g/l | Coconut Oil = 5.00 %w/w | Sodium Fluoride = 0.00 g/l
    Predicted yield: ~43.08 g/l citric acid

▶ OUTPUT: BioForge Optimization Protocol Card
    Baseline yield (no stimulants): 28.90 g/l
    Optimized yield: 43.08 g/l
    Improvement: +49.1%
    Published experimental validation: 43.08 g/l ✓ (exact match)

The protocol card is exported as a structured text document containing all optimal process conditions, model validation metrics, factor sensitivity ranking, and a reproducibility note — enabling any U.S. laboratory or manufacturer to implement the optimized protocol independently.

8. Connection to National Benefit

8.1 Reducing Biochemical Import Dependence

BioForge directly addresses the technical barrier that prevents U.S. agricultural waste streams from being efficiently converted into domestically produced biochemicals. By reducing the cost and timeline of fermentation process optimization — the rate-limiting step in moving from laboratory proof-of-concept to commercially viable production — BioForge makes domestic biochemical manufacturing more economically competitive against established foreign producers.

8.2 Open-Source Multiplier Effect

BioForge is being developed as a fully open-source tool under the MIT license. This ensures that its benefits extend beyond any single institution. Academic groups at land-grant universities, USDA Agricultural Research Service laboratories, regional biorefinery startups, and national laboratories can access and apply the framework at no cost. A single methodological tool that improves the efficiency of dozens or hundreds of independent research programs generates a national benefit substantially beyond what any single optimized bioprocess would achieve.

Federal Policy Alignment
BioForge's objectives are directly aligned with Executive Order 14081 (September 2022 — National Biotechnology and Biomanufacturing Initiative), which established a whole-of-government commitment to expand U.S. domestic biomanufacturing capacity, reduce dependence on foreign-produced biochemicals, and develop the scientific workforce and infrastructure needed to support a resilient domestic bioeconomy.
